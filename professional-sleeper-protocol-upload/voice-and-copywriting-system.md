# The Professional Sleeper Protocol: Voice & Copywriting System

## Purpose
This guide defines how The Professional Sleeper Protocol speaks across the product.

The voice must feel:
- Premium
- Calm
- Precise
- Psychologically intelligent
- Non-shaming
- Mature
- Human

This is not a hype app. It is a trusted companion for adults rebuilding sleep behavior with dignity.

## Voice Pillars

### 1) Calm Authority
Write with quiet confidence. No panic, no urgency theater.

Use:
- Measured language
- Specific observations
- Grounded recommendations

Avoid:
- Alarmist framing
- Exclamation-heavy tone
- Fear-based pressure

Good:
- "Your wake time drifted by 42 minutes this week. We’ll stabilize that first."

Bad:
- "Danger zone! Your schedule is all over the place!"

### 2) Respectful Precision
Be clear, specific, and concise. Respect the user’s intelligence.

Use:
- Concrete time references
- Behavioral language
- Short, exact sentences

Avoid:
- Vague inspiration
- Generic wellness clichés
- Over-explaining obvious points

Good:
- "Lights-out target: 23:15. Begin wind-down at 22:30."

Bad:
- "Try to sleep better tonight and listen to your body."

### 3) Non-Shaming Accountability
Acknowledge patterns without moral judgment.

Use:
- "What happened" not "what’s wrong with you"
- Forward-looking framing
- Agency-supportive wording

Avoid:
- Blame
- Moral language ("good/bad person")
- Infantilizing encouragement

Good:
- "You missed two check-ins. Let’s restart with a smaller evening target."

Bad:
- "You failed again. You need more discipline."

### 4) Psychological Intelligence
Treat resistance, inconsistency, and fatigue as expected human dynamics.

Use:
- Normalization without minimizing
- Friction-aware design language
- Small-step behavior framing

Avoid:
- Clinical diagnosis terms
- Pop-psych labels
- Overconfident certainty about motives

Good:
- "When your day runs long, bedtime decisions get expensive. We’ll reduce decision load."

Bad:
- "You self-sabotage because you fear success."

## Universal Style Rules
- Prefer plain, elegant language over jargon.
- Use contractions naturally ("you’re", "we’ll") for human tone.
- Keep punctuation restrained; avoid exclamation marks unless truly necessary.
- Never use sarcasm with user behavior.
- Keep sentences mostly short; vary rhythm for readability.
- Use second person ("you") and collaborative first person ("we").
- Prioritize behavior and context over personality judgments.

## Red Lines (Never Use)
- Childish gamification language: "streak warrior", "sleep points", "level up king"
- Hustle culture framing: "no excuses", "rise and grind", "outwork your fatigue"
- Shame triggers: "lazy", "weak", "undisciplined"
- Clinical diagnosis claims: "you are clinically insomniac" unless made by licensed clinician in clinical context
- Generic self-help filler: "you got this", "just be positive", "manifest better sleep"

## Content-Type Rules & Examples

## 1) Quiz Items
### Goal
Elicit accurate self-report with low defensiveness.

### Rules
- Ask one behavior per item.
- Use concrete time windows.
- Keep neutral wording; no loaded assumptions.
- Offer realistic answer options.

Good:
- "In the past 7 nights, how often were you in bed within 20 minutes of your planned bedtime?"
- "After 21:00, how many caffeinated drinks did you usually have?"

Bad:
- "Why can’t you stick to a simple bedtime?"
- "Do you ruin your sleep with bad habits every night?"

## 2) Theory Cards
### Goal
Teach one principle quickly, with practical relevance.

### Rules
- One core concept per card.
- Explain mechanism in everyday language.
- End with one immediate application.

Good:
- "Sleep pressure builds the longer you stay awake. Long late naps reduce that pressure at night. If sleep onset is slow, cap naps at 20 minutes before 15:00."

Bad:
- "Circadian homeostatic dysregulation is multifactorial and therefore complicated."

## 3) Feedback
### Goal
Reflect data clearly and guide the next best action.

### Rules
- Start with observation from actual behavior.
- Separate facts from interpretation.
- Recommend one prioritized adjustment.

Good:
- "You averaged 6h 12m sleep this week, with your shortest nights before early meetings. Next step: set a fixed pre-meeting shutdown alarm at 21:45."

Bad:
- "Not great. You need to take sleep seriously."

## 4) Reports
### Goal
Provide strategic insight without overload.

### Rules
- Lead with executive summary.
- Highlight trend, friction point, and priority action.
- Use sober language, not dramatic storytelling.

Good:
- "This week improved sleep regularity (+18%). Main friction: variable wake time on weekends. Priority for next week: keep wake time within a 45-minute range daily."

Bad:
- "Huge breakthrough week! You crushed it, but also crashed hard on weekends!"

## 5) Micro-Interventions
### Goal
Interrupt unhelpful patterns in the moment with minimal cognitive load.

### Rules
- Keep under 18 words when possible.
- Name immediate action.
- Match likely user state (tired, rushed, overloaded).

Good:
- "Close one tab, dim lights, and start your 10-minute shutdown."
- "If your mind is racing, write tomorrow’s top 3 tasks, then stop planning."

Bad:
- "Transform your life right now with this powerful ritual."

## 6) Failure Recovery
### Goal
Re-engage without shame after missed days, setbacks, or regressions.

### Rules
- Normalize disruption.
- Avoid autopsy language.
- Restart with the smallest viable commitment.

Good:
- "The routine slipped. That happens. Tonight we only protect wake time for tomorrow."
- "You’re not behind. Re-entry plan: 1 check-in, 1 wind-down cue, done."

Bad:
- "You broke your streak. Start over from zero."

## 7) Buttons
### Goal
Make actions clear, calm, and intentional.

### Rules
- Use explicit verbs.
- Keep labels short (2-4 words).
- Avoid hype or game metaphors.

Good:
- "Start wind-down"
- "Save tonight’s plan"
- "Review weekly report"

Bad:
- "Crush tonight"
- "Level up"
- "Fix me now"

## 8) Empty States
### Goal
Turn absence of data into a clear next action.

### Rules
- State what is missing.
- Explain value briefly.
- Offer one direct next step.

Good:
- "No evening check-ins yet. Add your first one tonight to generate your sleep pattern baseline."

Bad:
- "Nothing here... yet! Let’s goooo!"

## 9) Disclaimers
### Goal
Set boundaries responsibly without fear or legal overreach in tone.

### Rules
- Be plain and respectful.
- Distinguish coaching from medical care.
- State when to seek professional support.

Good:
- "This program supports behavior change and is not a medical diagnosis or treatment. If you have persistent sleep disruption, breathing pauses during sleep, or severe daytime impairment, consult a licensed clinician."

Bad:
- "We are not responsible for anything that happens if you don’t sleep well."

## 10) Motivational Text
### Goal
Sustain adherence through grounded encouragement.

### Rules
- Motivate through evidence and identity consistency, not hype.
- Emphasize process, not perfection.
- Keep tone mature and understated.

Good:
- "Consistency is compounding. The quiet nights are building capacity, even before you feel the full effect."
- "You kept the wake window stable three days in a row. That is the lever that changes next week."

Bad:
- "No pain, no gain. Winners wake at 5 AM."

## Language Patterns To Prefer
- "Let’s reduce friction."
- "Here’s the next lever."
- "This pattern is common under load."
- "Small adjustment, high return."
- "We’re optimizing reliability, not perfection."

## Language Patterns To Avoid
- "Hack your sleep fast"
- "Dominate your morning"
- "Don’t be lazy"
- "Failure"
- "Fix yourself"
- "Just try harder"

## QA Checklist Before Shipping Any Copy
- Is the tone calm and emotionally regulated?
- Is the message specific and behavior-based?
- Is any wording potentially shaming or moralizing?
- Does it sound like an intelligent adult speaking to another intelligent adult?
- Is the next action clear?
- Did we avoid clinical diagnosis and generic self-help filler?
- Would this still feel respectful on the user’s worst day?
