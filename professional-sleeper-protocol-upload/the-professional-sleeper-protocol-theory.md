# The Professional Sleeper Protocol — Theoretical Foundation

## 1. Executive summary
The Professional Sleeper Protocol (PSP) is a behavioral self-regulation system built around sleep as an outcome of daytime and evening regulation, not an isolated nighttime event.

Core thesis: **Sleep is not an isolated behavior; it is the visible result of a full-day regulatory system.**

In practice, PSP combines eight behavior pillars that shape biological sleep drivers (sleep pressure, circadian timing, autonomic arousal), cognitive load (attention fragmentation, stress carryover), physical readiness (movement, nutrition, recovery), and psychosocial regulation (connection, self-trust, identity).

The model is designed for healthy adults (18–40) who want better energy, focus, recovery, and consistency. It is not a medical diagnostic or treatment system. It should support reflective self-calibration, micro-experiments, and sustainable behavior change.

## 2. Product philosophy
### Why this is not just a sleep app
A pure sleep tracker is descriptive. PSP is architectural: it helps users design days that increase the probability of quality sleep and functional mornings.

### Why the day creates the night
Sleep onset and quality are strongly shaped by:
- Circadian cues (light timing, wake consistency, food/activity timing)
- Homeostatic sleep pressure (time awake, activity level, naps)
- Cognitive/emotional arousal (unfinished tasks, scrolling, isolation stress)
- Physiological load (late caffeine/alcohol/heavy meals, inactivity)

### Why small repeatable behaviors matter
Behavior change literature consistently shows that repeatable, low-friction actions outperform occasional heroic efforts. Tiny wins also build self-efficacy and identity congruence (“I do what I say I’ll do”).

### Why tone should be non-shaming, precise, and human
Shame-driven framing increases avoidance and all-or-nothing cycles. PSP should emphasize curiosity, calibration, and recovery speed after misses.

## 3. Scientific and behavioral foundations

### 3.1 Sleep and circadian science
**Key ideas**
- Sleep timing emerges from interaction of homeostatic drive (Process S) and circadian timing (Process C).
- Light is the strongest zeitgeber: bright light late night tends to delay phase; morning light tends to advance/stabilize phase.
- Caffeine can meaningfully impair sleep even when consumed 6 hours before bedtime (dose-dependent).
- Alcohol may shorten sleep latency in some people but degrades second-half sleep and REM architecture.
- Late/heavy meals and irregular eating windows can worsen sleep initiation/quality in many adults.
- Stable wake time is a major anchor for circadian regularity.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Prioritize wake-time consistency and morning light as first-line behavior levers.
- Include “last caffeine”, “last alcohol”, “last meal timing”, and “evening light hygiene” in check-ins.
- Emphasize sleep opportunity regularity over perfect bedtime precision.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often do you wake up at roughly the same time, even after a bad night?”
- “How often do screens/light keep your brain in ‘day mode’ close to bedtime?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- 7-day fixed wake-time window (±30 min).
- 10–20 min outdoor light within 60 min of waking.
- Caffeine cutoff (e.g., at least 8 hours before target bedtime).
- Last full meal 2.5–3.5 hours pre-bed on most nights.

### 3.2 Behavioral change and habit design
**Key ideas**
- Fogg: behavior occurs when motivation, ability, and prompt converge.
- Implementation intentions (“If X, then I do Y”) improve follow-through.
- Environment design and friction shaping often outperform willpower.
- Identity-based habits strengthen persistence under stress.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Each micro-protocol should specify cue, tiny action, fallback version, and context.
- Ask users to “pre-commit” contingency plans for likely disruptions.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often do you make the healthy choice easier than the default choice?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- “If it’s 22:30, then phone charges outside bed area.”
- “If I miss my planned workout, I do a 7-minute fallback walk.”

### 3.3 Self-efficacy and self-trust
**Key ideas**
- Bandura: mastery experiences are the strongest source of self-efficacy.
- Kept small promises create identity evidence; repeated self-betrayal weakens agency beliefs.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Recommend minimum viable commitments users can actually keep.
- Track completion streak quality (honest follow-through), not just quantity.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often do you trust your own plan by evening because you kept your morning promises?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- Set one non-negotiable daily promise requiring <10 minutes.

### 3.4 Avoidance, discomfort, and ACT/CBT principles
**Key ideas**
- Avoidance is negatively reinforcing: short-term relief, long-term narrowing.
- ACT emphasizes values-consistent action with discomfort present.
- Healthy discipline differs from self-punishment: the former is values-guided and sustainable.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Reframe “Do Hard Things” as safe discomfort training in service of values.
- Include “repair actions” after misses instead of penalty framing.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often do you do the useful thing before you feel ready?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- Daily 2-minute “approach rep” (one avoided task started for 2 minutes).

### 3.5 Attention, distraction, and cognitive fragmentation
**Key ideas**
- Task switching and interruptions carry measurable cognitive costs.
- Attention residue reduces quality of subsequent work.
- Notification-rich environments amplify reactive behavior and emotional scrolling.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Build routines around interruption control blocks.
- Treat digital design as behavior architecture (not moral weakness).

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often does your attention feel intact at day’s end rather than scattered?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- Two daily 25–45 minute notification-silent focus blocks.

### 3.6 Movement, exercise, and physical resilience
**Key ideas**
- Regular exercise improves subjective sleep quality and insomnia symptoms in many adults.
- Both aerobic and resistance training support cardiometabolic health and mood regulation.
- Sedentary time reduction matters even when formal exercise exists.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Include “movement snacks” and minimum daily movement floors.
- Position training as long-term autonomy, not aesthetics.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often did your body earn sleep today through movement?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- 8–10k step target or personalized equivalent.
- 3 strength sessions/week, scaled to level.

### 3.7 Nutrition and energy regulation
**Key ideas**
- Stable energy supports regulation; high-UPF patterns can destabilize appetite/satiety and may be linked to poorer sleep outcomes.
- Protein/fiber density improves satiety and reduces decision fatigue.
- Late heavy meals may interfere with sleep in many users.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Avoid purity language; use “signal quality” framing.
- Recommend fallback meals to prevent late-night chaotic eating.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often did your meals support stable energy instead of spikes and crashes?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- “Protein + fiber first” at first meal.
- Pre-decide 2 emergency meals for busy days.

### 3.8 Social connection and isolation
**Key ideas**
- Social connection quality predicts health outcomes; persistent isolation is a risk marker.
- Co-regulation through relationships affects stress load and behavioral resilience.
- Solitude can be restorative; isolation is involuntary disconnection.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Include small, realistic connection reps (message, call, brief check-in).
- Track isolation drift without pathologizing introversion.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often did you reach for people, not just your phone, when stressed?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- One intentional social contact per day (2–10 minutes).

### 3.9 Identity, motivation, and self-regulation
**Key ideas**
- Autonomous motivation is more stable than purely external pressure.
- All-or-nothing thinking drives collapse cycles after minor misses.
- Reflective review should function as laboratory, not tribunal.

**Practical implications for the app**
- Convert outcomes into identity evidence (“I am a person who repairs quickly”).
- Use weekly review prompts focused on learning loops.

**Examples of self-check questions**
- “How often did you recover quickly after a miss instead of quitting the day?”

**Possible micro-interventions**
- 3-line evening review: worked / failed / next tiny adjustment.

## 4. The 8-pillar model

### Pillar 1: Do Hard Things
#### Core idea
Train approach behavior toward safe, meaningful discomfort to reduce avoidance-based living.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Avoidance accumulates unfinished stressors, raises cognitive arousal at night, and erodes self-trust.

#### Key mechanisms
- Negative reinforcement of avoidance
- Values-based committed action
- Mastery experience accumulation

#### What low score may indicate
- High delay/procrastination under discomfort
- Relief-seeking loops (scrolling, numbing) replacing action

#### What high score may indicate
- Better stress tolerance
- Faster recovery after friction

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des începi lucrul important chiar dacă nu ai chef?
- Cât de des alegi disconfortul scurt în locul regretului lung?
- Cât de des amâni până când presiunea devine urgență?
- Cât de des te întorci la un task greu după ce ai evitat?
- Cât de des faci un pas mic chiar când ziua pare pierdută?
- Cât de des confunzi blândețea cu renunțarea?

#### Micro-interventions
- 2-minute start rule for one avoided task daily
- “One hard thing before noon” checklist
- Pre-write first action step night before
- 10-minute discomfort timer (single-task)
- Daily “repair rep” after one avoidance moment
- End-of-day note: “What did I approach today?”

#### Common traps
- Self-punishment disguised as discipline
- Extreme challenge that is unsustainable
- Equating rest with weakness

#### Tone guidance
Use courageous, non-heroic framing: brave, small, repeatable reps; no macho language.

### Pillar 2: The Night Before
#### Core idea
Evening is a landing protocol: reduce stimulation, close loops, and prepare next-day ease.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Late cognitive/emotional activation delays sleep onset and reduces sleep continuity.

#### Key mechanisms
- Light-mediated circadian effects
- Pre-sleep arousal regulation
- Prospective memory offloading

#### What low score may indicate
- Chaotic evenings, bedtime drift, unfinished-loop rumination

#### What high score may indicate
- Faster sleep onset, calmer mornings, lower decision fatigue

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des începi seara cu un plan de “aterizare”, nu de “încă o rundă”?
- Cât de des închizi ecranele la timp ca să-ți lași creierul să încetinească?
- Cât de des adormi cu mintea încă în “task mode”?
- Cât de des îți pregătești dimineața cu 5 minute în seara anterioară?
- Cât de des îți depășești ora de culcare fără motiv real?
- Cât de des seara ta îți susține somnul, nu îl sabotează?

#### Micro-interventions
- 30-minute wind-down window (same time)
- Write tomorrow’s top 1–3 tasks
- Put phone on charger outside arm’s reach
- Dim home lights 60–90 minutes pre-bed
- Last caffeine logged and capped earlier
- “Kitchen closed” cue after a chosen hour

#### Common traps
- Revenge bedtime procrastination
- Over-engineered routines impossible on busy days

#### Tone guidance
Frame as craftsmanship and recovery, not rigid perfection.

### Pillar 3: Start With Purpose
#### Core idea
First-hour behaviors set attentional and circadian direction for the day.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Morning anchors (wake consistency, light, first actions) stabilize rhythms and reduce drift.

#### Key mechanisms
- Circadian anchoring via wake time/light
- Implementation intention priming
- Early-win self-efficacy effects

#### What low score may indicate
- Reactive mornings, scattered priorities, late-night spillover

#### What high score may indicate
- Better day structure, stronger follow-through, cleaner sleep drive build

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des începi ziua la o oră relativ constantă?
- Cât de des te expui la lumină naturală în prima parte a dimineții?
- Cât de des îți alegi intenționat primul task important?
- Cât de des telefonul îți decide prima oră?
- Cât de des ai o dimineață care te calmează, nu te grăbește?

#### Micro-interventions
- Fixed wake window (±30 min)
- 10–20 minutes outdoor light after waking
- No social feed in first 20 minutes
- First action card prepared night before
- 5-minute movement primer

#### Common traps
- Turning mornings into unrealistic 90-minute rituals
- Confusing urgency with priority

#### Tone guidance
Encourage “strong start, humane pace.”

### Pillar 4: Train the Future Body
#### Core idea
Move today to preserve tomorrow’s capability, resilience, and sleep quality.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Exercise improves sleep outcomes, stress handling, and daytime energy regulation.

#### Key mechanisms
- Homeostatic sleep pressure support
- Mood/stress modulation
- Metabolic and functional adaptation

#### What low score may indicate
- Sedentary drift, low physiological readiness, fragile energy

#### What high score may indicate
- Better recovery capacity and long-horizon health behavior identity

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des îți antrenezi corpul pentru cine vrei să fii peste 10 ani?
- Cât de des ai mișcare planificată, nu doar accidentală?
- Cât de des rămâi prea mult timp nemișcat?
- Cât de des alegi varianta minimă când nu ai timp pentru varianta ideală?
- Cât de des simți că mișcarea îți îmbunătățește somnul?

#### Micro-interventions
- 3 strength sessions/week (20–45 min)
- 2 aerobic sessions/week
- 5-minute mobility daily
- 2–3 movement snacks during workday
- Minimum step floor per day

#### Common traps
- All-or-nothing training plans
- Using exercise as punishment for food

#### Tone guidance
Focus on capacity, autonomy, longevity; avoid physique moralizing.

### Pillar 5: Food as Medicine
#### Core idea
Eat to stabilize energy, satiety, and recovery signals, not to chase dietary purity.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Energy volatility and late heavy eating can worsen evening regulation and sleep quality.

#### Key mechanisms
- Glycemic stability and satiety signaling
- Meal timing-circadian interaction
- Decision fatigue reduction via defaults

#### What low score may indicate
- Reactive eating, low protein/fiber, late chaotic intake

#### What high score may indicate
- Steadier energy, fewer cravings, better evening control

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des mesele tale îți mențin energia stabilă câteva ore?
- Cât de des mănânci pe pilot automat când ești obosit sau stresat?
- Cât de des îți planifici o opțiune simplă pentru zilele aglomerate?
- Cât de des cina târzie îți afectează somnul?
- Cât de des alegi alimente care te ajută mâine, nu doar acum?

#### Micro-interventions
- First meal: protein anchor
- Add fiber source to two meals/day
- Pre-plan 2 fallback dinners
- Finish main meal earlier on most nights
- Keep trigger snacks out of immediate reach

#### Common traps
- Orthorexic/perfectionistic framing
- Extreme restriction leading to rebound eating

#### Tone guidance
Use “signal quality” language; no moral labels (“good/bad person”).

### Pillar 6: Kill Distractions
#### Core idea
Protect attention as a finite biological resource.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Fragmented attention increases stress carryover, unfinished-task load, and evening rumination.

#### Key mechanisms
- Attention residue
- Task-switching costs
- Cue-triggered habitual checking loops

#### What low score may indicate
- High reactivity, low deep work, compulsive checking

#### What high score may indicate
- More focused output, lower cognitive fatigue, calmer evenings

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des îți protejezi blocuri de focus fără notificări?
- Cât de des sari între taskuri înainte să finalizezi ceva important?
- Cât de des te trezești scrollând fără intenție clară?
- Cât de des îți alegi conștient când ești disponibil pentru întreruperi?
- Cât de des ziua ta mentală se simte coerentă, nu fragmentată?

#### Micro-interventions
- Notification batch windows
- One deep-work block/day (start small)
- Phone out of sight during focused work
- Single-tab rule for 20-minute sprints
- End focus block with “next step” note

#### Common traps
- “Dopamine detox” absolutism
- Unrealistic zero-distraction expectations

#### Tone guidance
Treat distraction as design problem, not character defect.

### Pillar 7: End Isolation
#### Core idea
Build reliable, human co-regulation and support.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Isolation can increase stress burden, emotional dysregulation, and behavior collapse risk.

#### Key mechanisms
- Social buffering of stress
- Accountability and identity reinforcement
- Emotional processing via connection

#### What low score may indicate
- Withdrawal loops, hidden overwhelm, reduced resilience

#### What high score may indicate
- Better emotional regulation and consistency

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des ceri sprijin înainte să ajungi la limită?
- Cât de des ai conversații reale, nu doar schimb de mesaje?
- Cât de des te izolezi când îți este greu?
- Cât de des oferi și primești sprijin în mod concret?
- Cât de des te simți conectat la oameni care contează pentru tine?

#### Micro-interventions
- Daily 2-minute check-in with one person
- One weekly intentional call/walk
- “Ask early” rule for one stuck area
- Share one weekly commitment with accountability partner
- Replace one scroll episode with connection action

#### Common traps
- Waiting for perfect mood before reaching out
- Performing “social productivity” without real connection

#### Tone guidance
Normalize vulnerability in small doses; respect introversion.

### Pillar 8: Build Self-Trust
#### Core idea
Self-trust is built through repeated alignment between intention and action.

#### Why it matters for sleep and life regulation
Without self-trust, plans are cognitively discounted, and consistency collapses under stress.

#### Key mechanisms
- Mastery feedback loops
- Identity evidence accumulation
- Fast repair after misses

#### What low score may indicate
- Repeated broken commitments, self-doubt, avoidance of planning

#### What high score may indicate
- Strong agency, realistic commitments, resilient consistency

#### Self-check item directions
- Cât de des faci ce ai spus că vei face, chiar în versiune minimă?
- Cât de des îți setezi promisiuni prea mari pe care apoi le rupi?
- Cât de des repari rapid după o zi ratată?
- Cât de des te bazezi pe tine când îți faci planul?
- Cât de des îți vorbești cu respect când greșești?

#### Micro-interventions
- One daily promise that must be keepable
- “Never miss twice” recovery rule
- Define minimum viable version of each habit
- End-day trust score (0–2) + one repair action
- Weekly evidence log: promises kept

#### Common traps
- Confusing self-criticism with accountability
- Overpromising during motivation spikes

#### Tone guidance
Use dignifying language: honest, steady, repair-focused.

## 5. Short quiz/self-check design
### Design principles
- Not a right/wrong quiz: it is a reflective mirror.
- 1–5 slider, 5–6 items per pillar.
- Mix positively and negatively keyed items.
- Reverse-score negative items.
- Output patterns, not pathology.

### Recommended scale labels
| Score | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Almost never true | Pattern is mostly absent |
| 2 | Rarely true | Inconsistent and fragile |
| 3 | Sometimes true | Mixed pattern |
| 4 | Often true | Usually present |
| 5 | Almost always true | Strong, repeatable pattern |

### Score interpretation
- 4.2–5.0: Current strength; maintain and mentor other pillars.
- 3.4–4.1: Functional but improvable.
- 2.6–3.3: Vulnerable; prioritize in next 7-day protocol.
- 1.0–2.5: Fragile; start with tiny actions and low-friction design.

### Identifying vulnerable pillars
Use the bottom 1–2 pillars, then check for dependency chains (e.g., `Kill Distractions` low often drags `Night Before` and `Build Self-Trust`).

### Generating 7-day micro-protocols
- Pick one primary pillar + one supporting pillar.
- Choose 3 tiny actions total.
- Define cue + minimum version + repair plan.
- Review on day 3 and day 7.

### Avoiding judgment
- Feedback language should describe patterns, not identity flaws.
- Always pair “what may be happening” with “one next doable action.”

## 6. Report design
### Suggested report structure
1. PSP Overall Score
2. Strongest Pillar
3. Most Vulnerable Pillar
4. Top 2–3 Behavioral Patterns
5. 7-Day Micro-Protocol (3 actions)
6. Pillar Score Table
7. Optional “Copy to ChatGPT” reflective summary
8. Short disclaimer

### Example interpretation style
- “Your strongest pillar is `Start With Purpose`: your mornings are creating structure.”
- “Your most vulnerable pillar is `Kill Distractions`: attention fragmentation may be leaking into evening overstimulation.”
- “For the next 7 days, run this minimal protocol…”

### Disclaimer text (short)
“This report is reflective and educational. It does not diagnose medical or mental health conditions and is not a substitute for professional care.”

## 7. Content voice guide
### Voice attributes
- Premium, calm, precise
- Psychologically intelligent
- Warm, mature, non-sentimental
- Anti-shame, anti-hustle

### Good vs bad wording examples
| Context | Good | Bad |
|---|---|---|
| Item wording | “Cât de des revii la rutină după o zi grea?” | “Ești disciplinat sau nu?” |
| Feedback | “Pattern suggests evening overstimulation; try a smaller wind-down anchor.” | “You failed your sleep hygiene.” |
| Failure | “A miss is data. Repair in the next 24 hours.” | “You broke the streak again.” |
| Discipline | “Discipline = promises you can keep.” | “No excuses. Push harder.” |
| Sleep safety | “If sleep problems persist or worsen, consider medical guidance.” | “This protocol fixes insomnia.” |

### How to talk about failure
Use “interruptions and repairs” language. Normalize misses as expected in behavior change.

### How to talk about discipline
Define discipline as consistency with humane standards, not intensity.

### How to talk about sleep problems safely
Avoid cure claims. Encourage professional help when persistent severe symptoms are present.

## 8. Research-backed source map

| Topic | Source | Supports | Evidence strength | Notes / cautions |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sleep regulation | Borbély (1982); Borbély & Achermann (2004 review) | Two-process model (homeostatic + circadian) | Strong conceptual foundation | Model is framework-level, not full clinical protocol |
| Circadian cues/light | NIH/NHLBI sleep-wake cycle guidance; light-timing reviews | Morning light as phase-advance/stabilizing cue; late bright light delays | Moderate-strong | Individual chronotype variation exists |
| Caffeine timing | Drake et al., 2013, J Clin Sleep Med | Caffeine up to 6h pre-bed can impair sleep | Strong for acute effect | Dose in study (400 mg) is high; sensitivity varies |
| Alcohol and sleep | Recent systematic reviews/meta-analyses (healthy adults, 2024) | Sleep architecture disruption, especially REM effects | Moderate-strong | Heterogeneity by dose, tolerance, sex, pattern |
| Sleep hygiene | CDC, NHLBI, AASM behavioral guidance | Consistent wake time, environment, evening habits | Moderate-strong consensus | Mostly guidance-level + mixed RCT quality by component |
| CBT-I principles | AASM clinical practice guidance; behavioral sleep medicine sources | Stimulus control, sleep scheduling, cognitive/behavioral mechanisms | Strong for insomnia treatment | PSP should borrow principles, not claim CBT-I treatment |
| Behavior change | Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) meta-analysis | Implementation intentions improve goal attainment | Strong | Effects vary by context and commitment quality |
| Habit architecture | Fogg Behavior Model (Stanford Behavior Design) | Motivation-Ability-Prompt design logic | Moderate (framework) | Practical model; less clinical-trial style evidence |
| Self-efficacy | Bandura (1977, 1997) | Mastery experience as core efficacy builder | Strong theoretical and broad applied evidence | Translational, not disease-specific |
| Avoidance/ACT | Hayes et al. (2006) ACT model review | Experiential avoidance, psychological flexibility, values action | Moderate-strong | Mechanism language must avoid overmedicalization in app |
| Attention costs | Rubinstein et al. (2001); Leroy (2009) | Task-switching costs, attention residue | Moderate-strong | Lab/work-context generalization needs careful wording |
| Exercise and sleep | Systematic reviews/meta-analyses (2020–2023) | Exercise improves subjective sleep and insomnia symptoms | Moderate-strong | Effect sizes vary by modality and baseline sleep issues |
| Physical activity baseline | WHO/CDC adult movement guidance | Health benefits of regular movement + less sitting | Strong consensus | Tailor dose to user baseline |
| Nutrition quality | UPF and sleep systematic reviews (2022+) | Associations between UPF intake and poorer sleep outcomes | Moderate (mostly observational) | Avoid causal overstatement |
| Social connection | Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010 meta-analysis | Social relationships linked to mortality risk | Strong (large meta-analysis) | Broad population data; not individual prognosis |
| Motivation quality | Ryan & Deci SDT (2000 onward) | Autonomous motivation and sustained behavior | Strong theoretical + applied evidence | Needs practical translation into app prompts |

### Popular experts and interpretation boundaries
- **Matthew Walker**: useful synthesis and communication; treat as interpretation layer, verify specific claims with primary sources.
- **Andrew Huberman**: practical protocols can be useful; classify as applied interpretation unless directly backed by high-quality studies.
- **Satchin Panda**: circadian biology and time-restricted eating research is influential; human outcomes vary by population/protocol.
- **Bryan Johnson-style optimization**: philosophy/motivation source; not itself evidence.
- **James Clear**: behavior communication framework; practical, not clinical evidence source.

## 9. Implementation implications
### What goes in “Ideea principală”
- One sharp pillar thesis in plain language.
- One mechanism sentence (“why this works”).
- One “start tonight/tomorrow” action.

### What goes in “Dive deeper”
- 3–5 evidence-backed mechanisms with brief caveats.
- Pattern examples (how this shows up in real life).
- Common trap + repair strategy.

### What goes in “Short quiz”
- 5–6 reflective items/pillar.
- Mix positive/negative statements.
- 1–5 sliders, no right/wrong framing.

### What goes in final report
- Pattern synthesis and next-week protocol.
- Practical, non-clinical, non-judgmental language.
- Explicit reminder: reflective, not diagnostic.

### What should stay out of the app
- Cure claims (“fixes insomnia”, “resets dopamine”).
- Overly technical biomarker detail for general users.
- High-friction routines that require perfect compliance.
- Shame-based language or moralized nutrition/exercise framing.
